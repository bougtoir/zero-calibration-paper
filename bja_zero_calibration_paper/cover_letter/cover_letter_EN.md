We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Pulse Pressure Accuracy as an Implicit Gain Validator: A Theoretical Framework for Zero-Calibration-Free Arterial Pressure Monitoring"** for consideration as a Special Article in the *British Journal of Anaesthesia*.

### Why this manuscript should be published in the BJA

This manuscript addresses a fundamental yet unexamined aspect of invasive arterial pressure monitoring: the theoretical basis for eliminating manual zero calibration through proper device design. We present an original theoretical framework demonstrating that if pulse pressure measurement is accurate, the transducer gain is implicitly validated, and that when combined with existing engineering solutions (catheter-tip MEMS sensors, barometric compensation, and self-calibrating reference cavities), all sources of DC offset can be eliminated by design — rendering manual zero calibration redundant.

We formalise this argument using Lin's Concordance Correlation Coefficient (CCC) decomposition into precision (r) and accuracy (Cb), showing that zero calibration corrects only the location shift (u) component of Cb while leaving the scale shift (v) — corresponding to gain error — entirely unaddressed. This analysis reveals a significant limitation of current validation methodology: Bland-Altman analysis alone can mask gain error when offset and gain errors coincidentally cancel, a finding we demonstrate through simulation (Table 2, Figures 5 and 8).

The BJA has a distinguished history of publishing foundational methodology in haemodynamic monitoring, including the seminal work by Bland and Altman on agreement analysis and the influential discussions on cardiac output monitor validation standards. Our manuscript extends this tradition by providing a rigorous theoretical basis for a design paradigm shift in arterial pressure monitoring — from routine manual calibration to calibration-free operation.

### Relevance to the BJA readership

Zero calibration of invasive arterial pressure transducers is a ubiquitous clinical procedure performed millions of times annually worldwide. Demonstrating that this procedure is theoretically eliminable through appropriate engineering design has direct implications for:

- Clinical workflow efficiency in operating theatres and intensive care units
- Patient safety (elimination of calibration-related errors)
- Medical device design and regulatory validation methodology
- The broader discussion of how haemodynamic monitors should be validated (CCC vs. Bland-Altman alone)
